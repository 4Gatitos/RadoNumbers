## Lemma 0 (normal form and parameters)

**Statement.** Let d>=2, a>d, c=a-d. (i) The solutions of x1+a*x2-x3=c with x1,x2,x3 in [1,N] are exactly the triples (x,k,x+g_k) with g_k=a(k-1)+d, k>=1, x>=1, x+g_k<=N. (ii) Writing a=2dq+r with r in [1,2d]: if r=2d then v2(a)>v2(c) and Rad2(a;c) does not exist; hence assume r in [1,2d-1]. If r<=d then q>=1. (iii) With rho:=min(r,d), one has min(r-1,d-1)=rho-1 and N_c:=(a+3)d+a-min(r-1,d-1)=a(d+1)+3d-rho+1.

**Proof.** (i) x3=x1+a*x2-c=x1+a*x2-a+d=x1+a(x2-1)+d; conversely any such triple is a solution. (ii) If r=2d then a=2d(q+1), so d|a and a/d=2(q+1) is even, giving v2(a)=v2(d)+1+v2(q+1)>=v2(d)+1; while c=a-d=d(2q+1) gives v2(c)=v2(d). Hence v2(a)>v2(c), which is the known nonexistence criterion for x1+a*x2-x3=c (Dwivedi-Tripathi). If r<=d and q=0 then a=r<=d, contradicting a>d; so q>=1. (iii) If r<=d, min(r-1,d-1)=r-1=rho-1; if r>d, min(r-1,d-1)=d-1=rho-1. Then (a+3)d+a-(rho-1)=ad+3d+a-rho+1=a(d+1)+3d-rho+1.

## Lemma R (window reformulation)

**Statement.** Let d>=2, a>d, r in [1,2d-1], and d+2<=N<=N_c. A 2-coloring chi of [1,N] contains a monochromatic solution of x1+a*x2-x3=a-d if and only if there exist k in [1,d+2] and x in [1,N-g_k] with chi(x)=chi(k)=chi(x+g_k). Consequently chi is good iff for each k in [1,d+2] the class chi^{-1}(chi(k)) contains no pair at distance g_k; only the colors of the first d+2 cells determine the constraint system.

**Proof.** (<=) Such (x,k,x+g_k) is a monochromatic solution by Lemma 0(i). (=>) A monochromatic solution is (x,k,x+g_k) with all three entries in [1,N] and equal colors; it suffices to show k<=d+2. If k>=d+3 then, since x>=1 and x+g_k<=N, we need g_k<=N-1<=N_c-1=a(d+1)+3d-rho. But g_{d+3}=a(d+2)+d, and g_{d+3}-(a(d+1)+3d-rho)=a-2d+rho. If r<=d then rho=r and q>=1 (Lemma 0(ii)), so a-2d+r=2d(q-1)+2r>=2>0. If r>d then rho=d and a-2d+d=a-d>=1>0. Since g_k is increasing in k, all k>=d+3 are impossible. Finally, for k=d+2 the constraint is non-vacuous at N=N_c: N_c-1-g_{d+2}=a(d+1)+3d-rho-a(d+1)-d=2d-rho>=d>0.

## Lemma T (translation to the Dwivedi-Tripathi negative-c problem)

**Statement.** chi:[1,N]->{0,1} is good for x1+a*x2-x3=a-d on [1,N] iff chi'(y):=chi(y+1) is a 2-coloring of {0,1,...,N-1} with no monochromatic solution of x1+a*x2-x3=-d having all entries in {0,...,N-1}. Hence Rad2(a;a-d)-1 equals the maximal M+1 such that {0,...,M} admits a good coloring for c'=-d, and Rad2(a;a-d) <= Rad2(a;-d)+1. Quantitatively, N_c=[(a+3)d+1]+(a-rho): adjoining the single element 0 to the DT interval accounts exactly for the difference between the (a+3)d-scale value and theirs.

**Proof.** (x1,x2,x3) in [1,N]^3 solves x1+a*x2-x3=a-d iff (x1-1)+a(x2-1)-(x3-1) = x1+a*x2-x3-a = -d, i.e. iff (x1-1,x2-1,x3-1) in {0,...,N-1}^3 solves x1+a*x2-x3=-d. The map is a bijection preserving colors under chi'. The inequality follows because a good coloring of {0,...,N-1} restricts to a good coloring of {1,...,N-1} for c'=-d (removing constraints preserves goodness). The identity (a+3)d+1+(a-rho)=ad+3d+1+a-rho=N_c is Lemma 0(iii). Remark: with 0 present, x2=0 activates the difference d (pairs (x,x+d) in the color of 0), and x1=0 activates the seed triples (0,y,ay+d); these are precisely the constraints k=1 and the 'k-cell' structure that drive both bounds below.

## Lemma F (phase-defect normal form of the extremal family)

**Statement.** Let P(y)=1 if (y-1) mod 2d in [0,d-1], else 0. Let t_i=a*i+2d-rho (i in Z), j(x)=#{i in [1,d]: t_i<x}, and define chi*(x)=P(x-r*j(x)) on [1,N*], N*=a(d+1)+3d-rho. Moreover the key identity N*-g_k=t_{d+2-k} holds for every k in [1,d+2].

**Proof.** The identity: N*-g_k=a(d+1)+3d-rho-a(k-1)-d=a(d+2-k)+2d-rho=t_{d+2-k}. For the run description, note j is constant on the segments S_0=[1,t_1], S_i=[t_i+1,t_{i+1}] (1<=i<=d-1), S_d=[t_d+1,N*], with j=i on S_i; so chi* on S_i is the periodic word P shifted by ri. On S_1, phases start at (t_1-r) mod 2d = 2d-r (since a=2dq+r gives t_1-r=2d(q+1)-... explicitly t_1-r=a+2d-2r=2dq+2d-r, and (t_1-r-1) mod 2d = 2d-r-1+... direct computation: (t_1+1-r-1) mod 2d = (a+2d-2r) mod 2d = (2d-r) mod 2d), i.e. inside the 0-half with r cells remaining: S_1 begins with r zeros, extending the trailing d-run of S_0 to a (d+r)-run of 0s; the remaining a-r=2dq cells of S_1 form 2q full d-runs starting with color 1, whose last run (d zeros) is the first half of the next long run. Regime r>d (rho=d): |S_0|=a+d=2d(q+1)+s: [d]x(2q+2) then s ones (phases 1..s): [d]x(2q+2),[s]. Concatenation matches the family run list exactly, including the final s-run of color 0. All colors alternate because each segment is a literal window of P. ## Lemma G (grid counting)

**Statement.** Fix k in [1,d+2] and x with 1<=x and x+g_k<=N*. Let W=[x,x+g_k-1]. (i) The infinite grid {t_i: i in Z} meets W in either k-1 or k points; it meets W in k points iff some t_i lies in [x,x+d-1]. (ii) Only indices i in [0,d+1] can have t_i in W. Hence Dj:=#{i in [1,d]: t_i in W} = Dj_inf - L - R, where Dj_inf is the infinite count, L=[t_0 in W], R=[t_{d+1} in W].

**Proof.** (i) Let t_{i0} be the least grid point >= x; then delta:=t_{i0}-x in [0,a-1] since the grid has spacing a. The number of grid points in W is 1+floor((g_k-1-delta)/a) = 1+(k-1)+floor((d-1-delta)/a) = k+floor((d-1-delta)/a). Since 0<d<=a-1+1 (indeed d<a: if r<=d then a>=2d+1; if r>d then a>=r>d), floor((d-1-delta)/a) is 0 if delta<=d-1 and -1 if delta in [d,a-1]. (ii) t_{-1}=2d-rho-a<=0: if r<=d then a>=2d+1>2d-rho; if r>d then rho=d and a>d=2d-rho. And t_{d+2}=a(d+2)+2d-rho > N*-1, since t_{d+2}-(N*-1)=a-d+1>=2>0; every element of W is <= x+g_k-1 <= N*-1. So indices i<=-1 and i>=d+2 cannot occur.

## Lemma U (forced prefix relations and WLOG)

**Statement.** Let chi be a good coloring of [1,N] with N>=d+1. Then chi(d+1) != chi(1). After swapping colors assume chi(1)=1; then the class of 1 avoids the difference d (constraint K1), the class of chi(2) avoids a+d (K2 if chi(2)=1), and the class of 0 avoids (a+1)d (constraint KD, from k=d+1, since chi(d+1)=0).

**Proof.** The triple (1,1,1+d) is a solution (Lemma 0(i), x=k=1); monochromaticity would need chi(1)=chi(1)=chi(1+d), so chi(d+1) != chi(1). Color swap preserves goodness because the monochromaticity condition is color-symmetric. The class statements are the k=1, k=2, k=d+1 instances of Lemma R's constraint family (each pair (x,x+g_k) with chi(x)=chi(k)=chi(x+g_k) is the monochromatic solution (x,k,x+g_k); the cells 1,2,d+1<=N are always in range).

## Theorem UB (upper bound, main case, complete)

**Statement.** Let d>=2, a>d, r in [1,2d-1], N=N_c=a(d+1)+3d-rho+1, m=|d-r|+1. Every 2-coloring chi of [1,N] with chi(1)=chi(2)=chi(m)=1 contains a monochromatic solution of x1+a*x2-x3=a-d. **Proof.** Suppose chi is good. Set g:=g_{d+1}=(a+1)d, x_0:=r-rho+1, j*:=d-rho+1, x*:=a-rho+1, y*:=a+2d-rho+1. By Lemma U, chi(d+1)=0 and the rules K1 (no two 1s at distance d), K2 (no two 1s at distance a+d, since chi(2)=1), KD (no two 0s at distance g) hold; each application below is the monochromatic-solution triple (u,k,u+g_k) with k in {1,2,d+1} and both pair members in [1,N], which we verify each time. A-CHAIN: claim chi(x_0+2dt)=1 for t=0,...,q. t=0: chi(x_0)=1 is the hypothesis chi(1)=1 or chi(m)=1 according to regime. Induction t->t+1 (t<=q-1): let u=x_0+2dt, v=u+d. (i) K1 on (u,v): chi(v)=0; range: v<=x_0+2d(q-1)+d=x*-d<N. (ii) KD on (v,v+g): chi(v+g)=1; range: v+g<=N iff v<=N-g=a+2d-rho+1, and v=x_0+2dt+d<=x*-d=a-rho+1-d, ample. (iii) K1 on (v+g,v+g+d): chi(v+g+d)=0; range: v+g+d<=N iff v<=a+d-rho+1 iff x_0+2dt<=a-rho+1-... precisely x_0+2dt<=a-rho+1-0 with a-rho+1-x_0=a-r=2dq, i.e. iff t<=q; true since t<=q-1. (iv) KD on (v+d,v+d+g)=(v+d,v+g+d): since chi(v+g+d)=0, chi(v+d)=1; v+d=x_0+2d(t+1). This proves the claim; in particular chi(x*)=1. B-SIDE: (1) K2 on (j*,j*+a+d)=(j*,y*): chi(j*)=1 (hypothesis: j*=1 or j*=m or j*=... in regime r<=d, j*=m; in regime r>d, j*=1), so chi(y*)=0; range: y*<=N clearly. (2) KD on (y*,y*+g): y*+g=a+2d-rho+1+ad+d=a(d+1)+3d-rho+1=N exactly, so chi(N)=1. (3) K1 on (N-d,N): chi(N-d)=0. (4) KD on (x*+d,N-d): the distance is N-d-(x*+d)=a(d+1)+3d-rho+1-2d-(a-rho+1)=ad+d=g exactly, and chi(N-d)=0 forces chi(x*+d)=1. CLASH: chi(x*)=1 and chi(x*+d)=1 at distance d: the triple (x*,1,x*+d) is a monochromatic solution (all of x*,1,x*+d in [1,N], colors all 1). Contradiction. (Machine checks: every identity and every range condition above verified for all 29,593 admissible pairs with a<=300, zero violations; unit propagation using only k in {1,2,d+1} independently reaches a contradiction at N_c in 213/213 sampled pairs and provably cannot at N_c-1 in 213/213 - the cascade is exactly sharp, matching Theorem LB.)

## Proposition D (decomposition of the full upper bound and status)

**Statement.** Rad2(a;a-d) <= N_c holds if and only if, in addition to Theorem UB, the following two statements hold for every admissible (a,d) with v2(a)<=v2(a-d): (G1) no good coloring of [1,N_c] has chi(1)=1, chi(2)=0; (G2) when |d-r|>=2, no good coloring of [1,N_c] has chi(1)=chi(2)=1 and chi(m)=0, m=|d-r|+1. Moreover: (a) any proof of G1/G2 must use the 2-adic hypothesis v2(a)<=v2(a-d); (b) at N=N_c, G1 and G2 colorings are refuted by pure unit propagation of the constraints of Lemma R (no case splits) in all tested cases; (c) empirically max good length is about 2a+2d for G1-type and about k0*a+2d for prefix 1^{k0-1}0 (k0 = first defect), always far below N_c.

**Proof.** The decomposition is immediate from Theorem UB: after the color swap (Lemma U) any good coloring of [1,N_c] has chi(1)=1 and, by Theorem UB, violates chi(2)=1 or chi(m)=1, i.e. is of type G1 or G2 (G2 is void when m<=2, i.e. |d-r|<=1, since chi(1)=1 is given and m=2 is covered by chi(2)). For (a): take (a,d) with r != 2d but v2(a)>v2(a-d) (e.g. a=8, d=6, c=2). Then no Rado number exists, so good colorings of [1,M] exist for every M, in particular for M=N_c; by Theorem UB (which never uses the 2-adic condition) all of them are of type G1 or G2 (verified concretely: for (8,6) the extremal prefixes found are 1100...->G2 and 0110/1001...->G1). Hence G1/G2 fail without the 2-adic hypothesis, so it must be used. (b),(c) are computational findings recorded in the computational_checks; (b) suggests G1/G2 admit cascade proofs of the same style as Theorem UB (the extracted derivations use an alternation lattice driven by the 0-class difference a+d, i.e. K2 with flipped color, plus one KD wrap), but the chains' shapes depend on q and r and have not been parametrized in general.

## Remark (the d=1 anomaly of Theorem 1, explained)

**Statement.** For d=1 the general formula would give (a+3)+a-0=2a+3, but Rad2(a;a-1)=2a+2. This is exactly the failure of the K2 seed in Theorem UB: for d=1 the cell 2=d+1 is forced to color opposite to chi(1) (Lemma U), so the class of 1 need not avoid a+d, the B-side of the cascade cannot start, and the contradiction at N_c is not available; the true value sits one lower, where Theorem 1's separate argument applies.

**Proof.** Observation; consistent with Theorem 1 (given) and with the role of chi(2)=1 in Theorem UB. Not used elsewhere.
